Cold Call
Talk Track
Get to a yes or a qualified no — fast
Internal Sales Resource

The goal of a cold call is simple: qualify fast, create enough curiosity to earn a discovery call, and book the next step.

Keep it short. Get them talking. Don't pitch — ask.

1
Greeting
Open

Hey <First name>, it's Sadid from boilerroom — how's it going?

If they ask where you're from:

Sadid from boilerroom — we're an outbound platform.

Context hook

I know you're heading up <role / team / company context>, I was curious if —

2
Trap
Qualify

Pick the one that fits best — you're checking if outbound pipeline is on their radar at all.

  • Outbound sales is important to you?
  • Building the outbound motion is important to you?
  • Building outbound pipeline is important to you?
  • Is pipeline generation something you're focused on?
  • Is outbound pipeline something you look after?
  • I know you guys are early stage — curious if you're thinking about generating outbound pipeline?
If they say "Yes" →

Oh okay sick — what does that look like right now?

Is that something you guys are just building out or is it set in stone?

3
Bucket
Pain

Match the bucket to their situation. Frame your pain statement around what matters to them.

Larger / more established sales teams

Usually when I talk to heads of sales, they say they want their reps to increase selling time without spending new money — but still get predictable outbound pipeline and visibility into it.

Curious — to what extent do you also resonate with that?

4
Pitch
Connect
Rule

Don't recite features. Pitch back the exact pains you just uncovered — mirror their language, connect what boilerroom does directly to what they told you.

So based on what you just said about <their specific pain> — what we do at boilerroom is consolidate prospecting, sequencing, dialing, and follow-up into one workflow. So instead of your reps stitching together four different tools, everything runs from one place.

The reason teams come to us is they want more pipeline without adding headcount or tools — and they want visibility into what's actually working.

Does that sound like it maps to what you were describing?

5
Common objections
Handle

An objection generally means your prospect didn't understand, genuinely doesn't need it, or isn't the right person. Your job is to figure out which one.

Most objections on a cold call are smokescreens — reflexive brush-offs to get off the phone. A genuine objection has a real, specific reason behind it.

A smokescreen looks like: vague, immediate, no detail — "not interested," "we're all set," "send me an email." The prospect hasn't actually thought about it.

A genuine objection looks like: specific, reasoned, unprompted detail — "we just signed a 2-year deal with Outreach last month" or "we don't do outbound at all, our model is fully inbound."

The best way to get past either is to poke at it — ask a clarifying question that forces them to either reveal it's a smokescreen or confirm it's real. If it's real, respect it and move on.

"Send me an email"
Response

For sure, I'll send something over. Just so I don't waste your time with something irrelevant — is outbound pipeline actually something you're working on, or is the timing just off right now?

Why it works

Forces them to actually engage. If it's a smokescreen they'll usually open up. If it's genuine, you get a real answer and can send something actually relevant.

"We already use [Apollo / Outreach / HubSpot]"
Response

Totally — a lot of the teams we work with were using those too. Are you happy with it, or is there anything you wish it did better? I'm curious what your workflow looks like.

Why it works

Almost no one is 100% happy with their current stack. This gets them talking about gaps without you having to pitch against their existing tool.

"Not interested"
Response

Completely fair — can I ask what specifically? Is it that outbound just isn't a priority right now, or is it more that you haven't seen something solve this well?

Why it works

"Not interested" with zero context is almost always a smokescreen. This forces specificity — if they can't give you a reason, they're brushing you off, not genuinely uninterested.

"We don't have budget"
Response

Totally get it — is that more of a timing thing where budget opens up later, or is outbound genuinely not a priority this year?

Why it works

Separates "no budget ever" from "no budget right now." If it's timing, keep the door open. If outbound isn't a priority, that's a genuine objection — don't force it.

"I'm not the right person"
Response

Appreciate you saying that — who would own this on your end? Is that something you could point me toward, or even shoot me a quick intro?

Why it works

Sometimes true, sometimes a dodge. Either way you get a referral or a confirmation it's a smokescreen. A warm intro from this person is far better than a cold call to the right one.

"We're not focused on outbound right now"
Response

Makes sense — is that more of a "we tried it and it didn't work" situation, or more of a "we haven't gotten to it yet"?

Why it works

"Tried it and it didn't work" is an opening — that's a pain point. "Haven't gotten to it yet" means it's on the roadmap. Either way you have something to work with.

Run a tight disco and earn the right to a demo
Internal Sales Resource

If the prospect is qualified, the next step of the disco is to get them on a demo.

The disco should provide enough value and intrigue for the prospect to want to see a demo. Give them a reason to want to see you again.

1
Setting the stage
Open

(Greetings and small talk)

Great, so I think the best place to start would be to understand what you're trying to accomplish — <give context on your product and company>

If at the end of the call we're not the right solution for you, I'll also be honest and point you in the right direction.

If what you're trying to do is a good fit, we'll set up a technical demo on a separate call so I don't waste your time — does that all sound good?

2
Discovery questions
S Current Situation
  • What is your current situation?
  • How much have you explored so far?
  • What tools are you using in these workflows today?
P Problem
  • What challenges have you run into with what you've tried so far?
I Impact
  • Of all the things you are working on, how would you prioritize this? Number 1? Number 10?
  • Why is this being prioritized now? What are the potential negative impacts if you don't address this?
  • How does this purchase align with your team's current priorities?
  • If these challenges are not addressed, what negative consequences would you anticipate?
N Benefits / Ideal Situation
  • What would an ideal solution/workflow look like to you?
  • What positive (measurable?) business outcomes are you hoping to achieve?
  • What would it mean for the business if you implement this and it is a home run?
3
Recap pain points

Ok great, just to make sure we are on the same page, you want <give brief recap>. Is there anything I am missing?

(let them answer)

4
Communicate value

Great, to share more about <Company> — <share more about the product and company>

People work with us for X reasons: <share what kinds of companies work with you>

Does that all make sense and are there any questions I can answer?

5
Q&A session

(Let them ask questions, provide value!)

6
Schedule the demo
Close

Great! So the next step is to set up a demo. The demo will go over X things — <share what the demo will go through>

Locking in a time

How is tomorrow at 2pm PST? (Try not to go more than 2 days out — nail down a definitive time and send a calendar invite immediately.)

Just sent the invite! Can you see if you got it at the right time? (Wait for them to confirm.)

Great! I'm super excited for the demo tomorrow. If you have any questions ahead of time just email me — we're happy to help. Have a great rest of your day!

Demo
Talk Track
Run a focused demo that ends with a clear next step
Internal Sales Resource

The demo is typically a huge aha moment for end users. The more end users on the call, the better — if they like what they see they will be internal champions.

Decision makers and non-end users might be less interested in the tech and will want to understand pricing, privacy, and security.

1
Setting the stage
  • Go through current situation + confirm this is correct
  • Go through desired outcome + confirm this is correct

Ask: "Is this all correct? Has anything changed since we last chatted?"

2
Running the demo
💡 Delivery tip

When it's the first time someone is seeing <your product>, they can easily get overwhelmed. Go slow, pause a lot, speak clearly, and use your mouse pointer to highlight exactly what you're talking about.

💡 Engagement tip

Pause at every stage to ask if they have questions. Prod them hard — by default they'll say no, but getting them to ask 1 question makes them progressively more engaged. The goal is to surface all product questions before you get into commercials.

Prompts to surface the last few questions
  • So now we're at the end of the demo — great time for any product or technical questions. Do you have any on how you'd use <your product>?
  • Does it all make sense from a technical perspective what you need to do to integrate?
  • Does it all make sense from a product perspective what the user experience will be like?
  • OK, so no more product or technical questions before we move on?

Wrapping up
If hesitation
When to use this section

Use this when the prospect says "they have to talk internally" or is punting a decision.

If you sense hesitation
  • Call it out: I sense some hesitation around (perceived hesitation) — how are you feeling about it?
  • Prod them: Do you see a case where you wouldn't move forward?

<Handle the hesitation / objection>

If you're running out of time and still sense hesitation
  • Would it be helpful to set up some time to walk you through (perceived hesitation)?
    • Pricing: Walk through the build vs buy — share why companies like <companies you work with> chose us over building in-house.
    • Security: Walk through our security posture, how we handle data, and answer any remaining questions.
Set up a check-in call

Would you be open to me setting up a quick check-in call to answer any questions that come up after you chat internally? If you guys chat and it doesn't make sense to move forward, that's not a problem — we can always cancel the call.

5
Going for the close
Close

Ask: "Do you want to move forward with a trial?"

Don't say anything and sit in silence until they answer. It might feel awkward — wait it out.

Two possible outcomes
  1. Handle the objections they express (see section above) and set up a follow-up meeting
  2. They say yes — you're done!
